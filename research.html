The genomic architecture of speciation

Laupala crickets show the highest speciation rate of all arthoropods for which these rates have been documented. This radiation is thought to be driven by geographic isolation and rapid divergence in acoustic and chemical mating behavior. In the Shaw lab, we're interested in understanding the genomic architecture of speciation by mating behavior divergence. For example, how many genes contribute to phenotypic divergence between closely related species pairs, what are the quantitative effects of those genes, and how are they distributed across the genome? Do structural variation and recombination rate heterogeneity contribute to reproductive isolating barriers? Are genomic architectures similar across repeated divergence events? And what are the genes underlying phenotypes that keep closely related population apart and maintain reproductive barriers between species? We use quantitative trait loci (QTL) mapping and related techniques to address these questions using species across the Laupala phylogeny. 

Example of QTL scan result. The red curve indicates the strength of association with the phenotype under study, pulse rate of the male song in this case, across the genome, 7 autosomes and the X chromosome. Where the red curve peaks above the horizontal line (significance threshold), there is a genomic locus underlying phenotypic variation.

Hawaiian Dimensions - Population genomics and microbiome

In collaboration with UC Berkely, we work on a research project that integrates ecological and evolutionary timescales in adaptive radiations of Hawaiian arthropods. This integrative approach is contingent on the dynamic nature of the Hawaiian volcanic island archipelago, which allows us to swap time for space: the varying age and linear evolutionary order of the Hawaiian volcanoes prvoides a snap-shot of change along an evolutionary timeline across the populations of Hawaiian crickets that inhabit different volcanoes. We combine behavioral variation, population genetics, and microbiome analysis to examine divergence of older and more recent population of Laupala cerasina, an endemic to the big island of Hawaii that is found on 4 volcanoes of varying age, geology, and vegetation.
