Kerry L. Shaw, professor. kls4<at>cornell.edu

My lab studies the speciation process in one of the fastest speciation arthropod radiations in the world: the Laupala swordtail crickets endemic to Hawaii. Our approaches vary from behavioral and quantitative genetics, to examining levels of pre and postcopulatory isolation, to unraveling and the phylogeographic history of recently radiated populations.

Mingzi Xu, postdoc. mx52<at>cornell.edu

I use quantitative trait locus (QTL) mapping approaches to study the genetic basis of variation in acoustic mating signals and mate preferences between the crickets Laupala kohalensis and L. paranigra.  

Thomas Blankers, postdoc. tb492<at>cornell.edu

I am interested in the evolutionary genetics of speciation. I use comparative quantitative genetics and population genetics approaches to understand the relationship between genetic architecture of speciation phenotypes, the geographic and demographic context of genetic evolution, and the effects of (sexual) selection. 

Glenn Stamps, Graduate student. gfs52<at>cornell.edu

I examine the variation in cuticular hydrocarbon (CHCs) and their role in premating isolation in Laupala. 

Hayden Waller, Graduate student. whw84<at>cornell.edu

I am interested in behavioral isolation, especially at the mechanistic level of analysis. I will be using functional genomic approaches to better understand the role of specific candidate genes underlying behavioral variation to uncover the phenotypic effects of individual genes in the polygenic mating behavior in Laupala.
